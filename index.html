When a Spark Becomes a Wildfire

Every year tens of thousands of wildfires burn across the United States. Most are small. Some destroy entire towns. This page walks from the national trend down to a random-forest risk model for a single address — built from real 2026 satellite detections, incident reports, and weather data.

01 · The national picture

How many wildfires happen every year?

Since 1983, the National Interagency Coordination Center has tracked every wildfire reported by federal, state, and local agencies. The count swings year to year with weather and drought — but it has rarely dropped below 45,000 fires a year, and some years top 90,000.

02 · The damage

Fewer fires don't mean less damage

The number of fires and the acreage they burn don't move together. 2023 had a below-average fire count but also the lowest acreage burned in over a decade; 2020 and 2015 both burned over 10 million acres. 03 · When it goes wrong

A few fires that changed everything

Acreage isn't the only measure of a disaster. Some of the most destructive U.S. wildfires were small by area but catastrophic in human and economic cost.

What actually drives the risk score?

We used SHAP-style Shapley-value attribution to ask, for the random forest's predicted fire probability: how much did each input push the prediction up or down, on average, across a held-out sample of days?
